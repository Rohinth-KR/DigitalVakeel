MICRO, SMALL AND MEDIUM ENTERPRISES DEVELOPMENT (MSMED) ACT, 2006
====================================================================

OVERVIEW
--------
The Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises Development Act, 2006 (MSMED Act 2006) was enacted by the Parliament of India to facilitate the promotion, development and enhancement of competitiveness of micro, small and medium enterprises. The Act came into force on October 2, 2006.

The Act provides a legal framework for recognition of the concept of "enterprise" (as distinct from the earlier narrower concept of "industry") and integrates the three tiers of micro, small and medium enterprises. It also provides for a statutory consultative mechanism at the national level with balanced representation of all sections of stakeholders, with a mandate to examine factors affecting the promotion and development of MSMEs and review policies and programmes of the Central Government.

CHAPTER V — DELAYED PAYMENTS TO MICRO AND SMALL ENTERPRISES
============================================================

SECTION 15 — LIABILITY OF BUYER TO MAKE PAYMENT
-------------------------------------------------
Where any supplier (micro or small enterprise) supplies any goods or renders any services to any buyer, the buyer shall make payment therefor on or before the date agreed upon between him and the supplier in writing or, where there is no agreement in this behalf, before the appointed day.

The "appointed day" means the day following immediately after the expiry of the period of fifteen days from the day of acceptance or the day of deemed acceptance of any goods or any services by a buyer from a supplier.

IMPORTANT: Notwithstanding anything contained in any agreement between the buyer and the supplier, the period agreed upon between them shall NOT exceed forty-five days from the day of acceptance or the day of deemed acceptance.

KEY RULE: The maximum payment period allowed under law is 45 DAYS from the date of acceptance of goods or services. Any agreement that specifies a longer period is void to that extent.

What this means for MSMEs:
- If you supply goods/services to a buyer, they MUST pay within 45 days
- Even if the contract says 60 days or 90 days, the legal limit is still 45 days
- The 45-day clock starts from acceptance of goods/services (or deemed acceptance)
- "Deemed acceptance" occurs when the buyer does not communicate rejection within 15 days

SECTION 16 — DATE FROM WHICH AND RATE AT WHICH INTEREST IS PAYABLE
--------------------------------------------------------------------
Where any buyer fails to make payment of the amount to the supplier, as required under Section 15, the buyer shall, notwithstanding anything contained in any agreement between the buyer and the supplier or in any law for the time being in force, be liable to pay compound interest with monthly rests to the supplier on that amount from the appointed day or, as the case may be, from the date immediately following the date agreed upon, at three times of the bank rate notified by the Reserve Bank.

INTEREST CALCULATION:
- Rate: THREE TIMES (3×) the RBI bank rate
- Current RBI bank rate: 6.5% per annum (as of 2024-2025)
- Therefore, applicable interest rate: 6.5% × 3 = 19.5% per annum
- Type: COMPOUND interest with monthly rests
- Starting date: From the day after the 45-day period expires
- The interest is calculated from the appointed day (day after payment was due)

EXAMPLE CALCULATION:
If invoice amount is ₹5,00,000 and payment is delayed by 20 days beyond the 45-day period:
- Annual rate = 19.5%
- Daily rate = 19.5% ÷ 365 = 0.0534% per day
- Interest = ₹5,00,000 × 0.000534 × 20 = ₹5,342
- Total due = ₹5,00,000 + ₹5,342 = ₹5,05,342

IMPORTANT NOTES ON INTEREST:
1. The interest rate cannot be reduced by any agreement
2. The interest is statutory — it applies automatically by operation of law
3. The buyer cannot argue that no interest clause was in the contract
4. The interest is COMPOUND with monthly rests, not simple interest
5. No court shall recognize any agreement that reduces this statutory interest rate

SECTION 17 — RECOVERY OF AMOUNT DUE
--------------------------------------
For any goods supplied or services rendered by the supplier, the buyer shall be liable to pay the amount with interest thereon as provided under Section 16.

The buyer is liable to pay:
1. The principal amount (original invoice value)
2. Compound interest at 3× RBI bank rate on the delayed amount
3. This liability exists regardless of any contractual terms

SECTION 18 — REFERENCE TO MICRO AND SMALL ENTERPRISES FACILITATION COUNCIL
----------------------------------------------------------------------------
Notwithstanding anything contained in any other law for the time being in force, any party to a dispute may, with regard to any amount due under Section 17, make a reference to the Micro and Small Enterprises Facilitation Council.

The Council shall conduct conciliation or take steps for arbitration.

KEY POINTS ABOUT SECTION 18:
1. Either party (supplier or buyer) can file a reference
2. The Council first attempts conciliation
3. If conciliation fails, the Council takes up arbitration
4. The arbitration is conducted under the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996
5. The Council's decision is binding
6. Every reference shall be decided within 90 days from the date of reference
7. The Facilitation Council has jurisdiction over disputes involving micro and small enterprises

WHO CAN FILE:
- Any micro enterprise (investment up to ₹1 crore, turnover up to ₹5 crore)
- Any small enterprise (investment up to ₹10 crore, turnover up to ₹50 crore)
- Must have valid Udyam Registration

SECTION 19 — APPLICATION FOR SETTING ASIDE DECREE, AWARD OR ORDER
-------------------------------------------------------------------
No application for setting aside any decree, award or order made either by the Council itself or by any institution or centre providing arbitration services shall be entertained by any court UNLESS the applicant (buyer) has deposited with it seventy-five per cent of the amount in terms of the decree, award or order.

This means: If a buyer wants to challenge the Council's decision in court, they must FIRST deposit 75% of the awarded amount. This protects MSMEs from frivolous appeals.

SECTION 20 — POWER OF CENTRAL GOVERNMENT
------------------------------------------
The Central Government may, by notification, direct that any provisions of the Act shall not apply to any class of enterprises or buyers. However, no such notification has been issued effectively removing any class from the Act's applicability.

SECTION 21 — BUYER'S DUTY TO FURNISH INFORMATION (FORM MSME-1)
-----------------------------------------------------------------
Every buyer who is required to get his accounts audited under the Income Tax Act, 1961, shall furnish the following information in his annual statement of accounts:
1. The principal amount and interest due to micro and small enterprises remaining unpaid beyond the appointed day
2. The amount of interest paid along with the principal amount
3. The amount of interest accrued and remaining unpaid
4. The total interest due and payable for the year (even if not actually paid)

This is done through FORM MSME-1 which must be filed with the Registrar of Companies.

IMPORTANT: This disclosure requirement acts as evidence because the buyer's own audited accounts will show the overdue amounts. This self-disclosure can be used by MSMEs in legal proceedings.

SECTION 22 — INTEREST NOT TO BE ALLOWED AS DEDUCTION
------------------------------------------------------
The amount of interest payable or paid by any buyer, under or in accordance with the provisions of this Act, shall not be allowed as a deduction under the Income-tax Act, 1961.

This means: The buyer cannot claim the interest paid to MSMEs as a tax deduction. This acts as a penalty — the interest payment is a real cost to the buyer with no tax relief.

ENTERPRISE CLASSIFICATION (UPDATED 2020)
==========================================

Micro Enterprise:
- Manufacturing/Services: Investment up to ₹1 crore AND Turnover up to ₹5 crore

Small Enterprise:
- Manufacturing/Services: Investment up to ₹10 crore AND Turnover up to ₹50 crore

Medium Enterprise:
- Manufacturing/Services: Investment up to ₹50 crore AND Turnover up to ₹250 crore

UDYAM REGISTRATION (Mandatory since July 1, 2020):
- Online registration at https://udyamregistration.gov.in
- Based on Aadhaar number
- No fees, no documents required for registration
- Udyam Registration Number format: UDYAM-XX-XX-XXXXXXX
- Must be obtained before filing any claims under MSMED Act

RIGHTS OF MSME SUPPLIERS
==========================
1. Right to receive payment within 45 days (Section 15)
2. Right to claim compound interest at 3× RBI rate (Section 16)
3. Right to file complaint at Facilitation Council (Section 18)
4. Right to file at MSME Samadhaan portal
5. Protection from frivolous appeals (75% deposit requirement under Section 19)
6. Evidence support through buyer's mandatory disclosure (Section 21)
7. Buyer cannot claim interest as tax deduction (Section 22)

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS
=======================
Q: "My contract says payment in 90 days. Does Section 15 still apply?"
A: YES. Section 15 overrides any contractual terms. The maximum period is 45 days by law.

Q: "Can the buyer refuse to pay interest saying there was no interest clause in the contract?"
A: NO. The interest under Section 16 is statutory — it applies automatically regardless of what the contract says.

Q: "I'm a medium enterprise. Can I use Section 18 to file at the Council?"
A: The Council under Section 18 is specifically for micro and small enterprises. Medium enterprises have other legal remedies available.

Q: "How long does the Council take to resolve disputes?"
A: Section 18 mandates resolution within 90 days of filing the reference.

Q: "What if the buyer doesn't have the money to pay?"
A: The buyer's inability to pay does not extinguish the liability. Interest continues to accrue. The MSME can pursue recovery through legal channels.
